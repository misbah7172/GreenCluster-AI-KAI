Current LLM inference solutions still show a strong architecture gap between performance-oriented systems and deployment-ready, resource-aware systems. Most popular runtimes are optimized for single high-end GPUs or cloud environments, while practical edge/lab clusters are heterogeneous and memory-constrained. This leaves a clear gap in methods that can distribute inference intelligently across mixed devices without assuming uniform hardware or expensive infrastructure. Your KAI idea directly targets this missing middle by proposing capacity-aware partitioning and distributed execution on commodity nodes.

There is also a scheduling gap in the literature and tooling: many systems use static or lightly adaptive policies, and very few treat fairness, cost, energy, and latency as a combined decision problem in real time. Existing works often optimize one objective (usually latency or throughput), but real deployments need balanced multi-objective control under changing workload pressure. KAI addresses this by combining FCIM/ADSA/ILP-style logic with telemetry-driven adaptation, which is a stronger research position than isolated heuristic scheduling.

A major operational gap exists in closed-loop control. In many published systems, monitoring is observational rather than actionable, meaning telemetry is collected but not consistently fed back into scheduling and migration policy updates. Your architecture closes this loop by explicitly connecting execution-plane telemetry to monitoring and controller modules, then feeding policy updates back to the scheduler. This is an important systems contribution because it turns monitoring from reporting into decision support.

Another gap is in end-to-end evaluation rigor. Many studies report speedups, but fewer provide comprehensive validation across latency, throughput, energy, fairness, and robustness with clear statistical treatment. Your proposal includes cross-validation, baseline comparisons, and multi-metric evaluation planning, which addresses the common reproducibility and comparability weakness seen in similar projects. This strengthens the work for academic review because it demonstrates not only system design, but also a measurable experimental methodology.

Finally, there is a sustainability and practicality gap. Existing approaches often under-emphasize energy lifecycle implications and deployment constraints such as Kubernetes complexity, network variability, and recovery overhead. Your proposal acknowledges these limitations and frames mitigation paths early, which improves credibility. In short, the gap analysis supports KAI as a relevant and timely research proposal: it is positioned at the intersection of distributed inference, adaptive scheduling, and energy-aware systems engineering where current solutions remain fragmented.
